**1) What happened:**

The "Operation Highland" by the Chinese threat group Velvet Ant involved compromising the authentication stack of a large organization's critical infrastructure. This cyber-espionage campaign persisted for a decade, starting in 2016, by targeting vulnerable internet-facing systems, before maneuvering into an isolated network without direct internet access. The attackers used modified GS-Netcat for remote access and crafted an execution path into the segregated environment via HTTP requests. They also trojanized PAM and OpenSSH components, intercepting and logging credentials and administrative activity, ensuring persistence even amidst changes in passwords and termination of sessions.

**2) Why it matters:**

This incident highlights the severe risk and potential long-term implications of sophisticated cyber-espionage activities. The ability of Velvet Ant to maintain access and control for a decade exposes significant vulnerabilities within critical infrastructure systems that are presumed secure due to isolation from the internet. Such breaches can lead to a profound and enduring impact on national security, economic stability, and the trustworthiness of digital infrastructure. Moreover, the complexity of remediation underscores the necessity for organizations to prepare effectively for advanced persistent threats and secure critical components.

**3) What actions should be taken:**

In response to insights from Operation Highland, organizations may focus on enhancing their cybersecurity posture by treating authentication components like PAM and OpenSSH as critical security assets. Implementing EDR, strict file integrity monitoring, and multi-factor authentication can help fortify defenses. Continuous monitoring for unauthorized modifications is crucial. Organizations should plan for offline recovery strategies, ensuring reliable and immutable backups are available. Furthermore, testing and validating backup restorations may prevent operational disruptions. Conducting breach and attack simulations can strengthen the detection capabilities of existing SIEM and EDR systems, reducing undetected attacks.
