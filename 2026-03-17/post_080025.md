Original Article: https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/uks-companies-house-confirms-security-flaw-exposed-business-data/

**1) What happened:**

A security flaw was identified in the UK’s Companies House WebFiling service, which allowed unauthorized access to data for five months. The vulnerability, reported by Dan Neidle and initially discovered by John Hewitt, permitted logged-in users to navigate back from their own dashboard and access other company dashboards without proper authentication. This exposure potentially included sensitive data such as management home addresses and email addresses of five million companies. Although no passwords were compromised, the flaw allowed unauthorized alterations to company records and data access, which has been reported to relevant authorities for further investigation.

**2) Why it matters:**

The incident highlights the vulnerabilities inherent in systems that manage large databases of sensitive information. Unauthorized access to business data can lead to identity theft, corporate espionage, or fraudulent activities, potentially damaging businesses' reputations and causing financial losses. Furthermore, the flaw's ability to expose sensitive details such as residential addresses raises significant privacy concerns. This underscores the importance of robust cybersecurity measures, especially for government services that handle critical data. The incident also serves as a reminder of the consequences of system updates without adequate security testing, especially in public-facing platforms.

**3) What actions should be taken:**

To address this vulnerability, the UK's Information Commissioner's Office and the National Cyber Security Centre should conduct a thorough investigation to determine if any breaches occurred and to prevent future incidents. Companies House should implement immediate security reviews and audits of their systems, ensuring robust protocols for testing updates before deployment. Organizations should enhance monitoring and logging mechanisms to detect unauthorized access promptly. Public and private organizations can use this incident as a case study to review their own security postures, focusing on access controls and user authentication processes, to safeguard against similar threats.
