**What Happened:** A team from the University of Toronto unveiled the GPUBreach attack, which exploits Rowhammer vulnerabilities in GPU's GDDR6 memory to escalate privileges and potentially take over systems. The attack allows an unprivileged CUDA kernel to gain abusive control over GPU memory, which can then be used to exploit memory-safety bugs in NVIDIA drivers, leading to a full system compromise without needing to disable IOMMU protections. This attack represents a significant advancement over previous Rowhammer threats by enabling privilege escalation and system-wide control.

**Why it Matters:** The GPUBreach attack is crucial as it transcends traditional data corruption threats by enabling attackers to gain root privileges even with IOMMU protections enabled, posing a significant security risk to systems using GPUs, particularly the prevalent NVIDIA RTX A6000. The demonstration of such vulnerabilities highlights a potential threat to AI development workloads and enterprise environments, exacerbating risk unless mitigations are adopted. It underscores a critical need for robust security measures beyond current protections, spurring urgency for hardware and software stakeholders to address these susceptibilities.

**Recommended Actions:** Stakeholders, particularly those utilizing GPUs in sensitive or high-stakes environments, should consider implementing enhanced security measures, such as enabling System Level Error-Correcting Codes where available. Security teams should monitor updates from NVIDIA and apply any new security advisories. Organizations are encouraged to assess their current hardware configurations and potentially upgrade to models with better inherent protections. Additionally, constant review of security practices and potential adoption of additional defenses such as firmware updates or specific Rowhammer mitigations for GDDR6 are recommended for maintaining robust security postures.
