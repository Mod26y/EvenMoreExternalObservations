Original Article: https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/fake-it-support-calls-on-microsoft-teams-push-etherrat-malware/

1) What happened:
Threat actors are exploiting Microsoft Teams by posing as IT support to deliver EtherRAT malware through a multifaceted attack campaign. This attack involves phishing emails, Microsoft Teams calls, and the use of legitimate remote-access tools. Victims are lured by a malicious PDF from phishing emails and deceived into granting remote access during a Teams call. Once access is granted, attackers install EtherRAT, a remote access trojan, which is capable of full control over infected systems and leveraging smart contracts to communicate with command-and-control servers. This campaign highlights the evolving threats targeting popular corporate communication tools.

2) Why it matters:
This evolving attack highlights cybersecurity challenges posed by remote communication tools, especially in corporate environments where security is paramount. With the increasing adoption of Microsoft Teams, cybercriminals exploiting such platforms can bypass traditional security measures, posing significant risks like data theft, unauthorized access, and business disruption. The use of EtherRAT, which offers extensive system control and persistent access, exacerbates potential damage. Understanding these methods is crucial as they demonstrate a shift towards more sophisticated and deceptive social engineering tactics, effectively compromising both user awareness and technological defenses.

3) What actions should be taken:
Organizations should enhance security awareness training to help employees recognize phishing attempts and suspicious activity on communication platforms. It's critical to implement multi-factor authentication (MFA) and restrictive permissions for remote access tools to mitigate unauthorized access risks. Security teams should deploy advanced threat detection tools that monitor for unusual activities and analyze external communication attempts, especially via services like Microsoft Teams. Regularly updating security policies and maintaining vigilance in audit logs for mismatches in tenant information can also help detect and deter similar attacks in the future. Microsoft’s added protections, like external caller warnings, can be leveraged to strengthen defenses.
