In this case, General Motors (GM) settled for $12.75 million with California over CCPA violations related to selling driver data without proper consent. GM allegedly collected and sold Californian drivers' driving and location data through its OnStar subsidiary and "Smart Driver" system to data brokers. The investigation, sparked by media reports, led to allegations of GM unlawfully retaining and repurposing data, which they profited from without driver consent, despite prior Federal Trade Commission (FTC) orders.

This settlement is significant as it highlights the importance of complying with privacy laws like the CCPA, emphasizing consumer rights over personal data. It serves as a powerful reminder to firms of the consequences of non-compliance, especially around data collection, retention, and third-party sharing. With the CCPA setting precedence, organizations must prioritize transparency and proper consent mechanisms to avoid hefty penalties and reputational damage.

Organizations should enhance their privacy compliance programs by assessing their data collection and sharing practices to ensure they align with legal requirements. This includes improving consumer consent processes, regularly deleting unneeded data, and conducting frequent privacy impact assessments. Companies can avoid potential liabilities by fostering a culture focused on data privacy protection. Additionally, implementing transparency in data handling processes can rebuild consumer trust, which is essential for long-term customer loyalty.
