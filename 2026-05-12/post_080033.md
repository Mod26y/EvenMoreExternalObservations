Original Article: https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/why-changing-passwords-doesnt-end-an-active-directory-breach/

1. **What happened:**  
The article discusses the limited effectiveness of merely changing passwords in active directory breaches. When passwords are reset, old credentials can remain valid due to cached password hashes and authentication protocols like Kerberos. Attackers exploit these gaps to maintain unauthorized access. For instance, cached credentials on devices and active Kerberos tickets can sustain access even post password change, and attackers can exploit service accounts or create forged tickets to bypass changes altogether. 

2. **Why it matters:**  
This issue is critical as passwords alone do not fully address the breach, making organizations vulnerable to ongoing exploitation. Attackers leveraging unexpunged active sessions, persistent service accounts, or altered ACLs can maintain or regain access, compromising sensitive information. Given the prevalence of stolen credentials in breaches, highlighted by Verizon’s report, understanding and mitigating these risks is essential for comprehensive incident response.

3. **What actions should be taken:**  
Organizations should implement thorough breach response strategies beyond password resets. This includes terminating active sessions and clearing Kerberos tickets via forced logoff or reboot, rotating service account passwords, and ensuring credential hygiene. Additionally, auditing the directory for changes in group memberships, ACLs, and privileged roles can help detect and remove any unauthorized access paths. Implementing tools that enforce secure password policies and expedite synchronization across environments can further protect against exploitation.
