Original Article: https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/student-hacked-taiwan-high-speed-rail-to-trigger-emergency-brakes/

1) What happened: In Taiwan, a 23-year-old university student, referred to as Lin, was arrested for hacking into the Taiwan High-Speed Rail (THSR) system using software-defined radio (SDR) communications. By transmitting a high-priority "General Alarm" signal, Lin managed to halt four trains for 48 minutes on April 5. He acquired necessary data and intercepted communications using SDR equipment, purchased online, to impersonate legitimate signals. The incident involved an accomplice and exploited the TETRA communication system, which had outdated security parameters unchanged for 19 years.

2) Why it matters: This incident highlights significant vulnerabilities in critical infrastructure systems, particularly those relying on outdated communications protocols like TETRA. It underscores the potential risks posed by technological advancements in hacking tools, such as SDR, that can be easily accessed and potentially exploited by individuals with malicious intent. The fact that a crucial service with millions of passengers annually was compromised emphasizes the need for consistent modernization and security evaluations to prevent similar disruptions, which can risk public safety and cause significant operational and economic impacts.

3) What actions should be taken: In response to this event, organizations operating critical infrastructure should prioritize reviewing and updating their security protocols, including rotating and securing communication parameters regularly. Investing in robust security measures and incident response strategies is crucial to mitigate potential threats. Additionally, implementing regular audits and penetration testing can help identify vulnerabilities before they are exploited. Raising awareness and training personnel against such threats should also be considered to bolster the system's defenses against future hacking attempts.
