The FBI has highlighted a significant surge in cyber-enabled cargo theft across the U.S. and Canada, projecting losses to hit $725 million by 2025. This represents a 60% increase from the previous year, driven by hackers and impersonators targeting high-value freight through access to freight brokers and carriers' systems. These criminals use spoofed emails and fake websites to infiltrate, post fraudulent load board listings, and reroute shipments to accomplices. Consequently, cargo theft incidents escalated by 18% last year, with the typical theft's value notably increasing due to the targeting of high-value loads.

This trend is critical because it highlights vulnerabilities in the transportation and logistics sector, affecting businesses that deliver, receive, and insure cargo. The rise in cyber-enabled theft not only leads to financial losses but also damages the trust and operational efficiency within the logistics chain. Moreover, it underscores the increasing sophistication of cybercriminals who meld traditional cargo theft tactics with cyber techniques, complicating mitigation and recovery efforts. The significant increase in theft value suggests that threat actors are becoming more adept at exploiting system weaknesses to steal high-value goods.

In response, transportation and logistics companies are encouraged to implement preventative measures such as verifying shipment requests through secondary channels, enforcing multi-factor authentication, and using two-factor validation for unexpected communications. Keeping comprehensive records of drivers and vehicles can facilitate tracking and recovery should thefts occur. If businesses suspect they are victims of such schemes, they should file reports with both the Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) and local law enforcement. Strengthening these security practices could mitigate risks and potentially prevent or reduce the impact of cargo theft.
