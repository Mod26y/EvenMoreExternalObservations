A Spanish court has issued precautionary measures against NordVPN and ProtonVPN to block 16 piracy websites facilitating unauthorized streaming of LaLiga matches. This ruling requires the providers to restrict access to dynamic IP addresses in Spain, without the opportunity for the VPN services to present a defense prior to the decision. The court's decision exemplifies an extension of liability of VPN providers under the EU Digital Services Regulation, treating them as facilitators of copyright infringement. This aligns with similar precedents in other European countries like France and reinforces the role of digital service providers in content protection.

This matters because it marks a significant step in holding VPNs accountable under EU regulations for enabling access to pirated content. This ruling underscores a growing trend where courts globally are seeking to involve intermediaries, like VPN providers, in combating digital piracy. It raises concerns about due process and potential overreach in judicial actions that can affect how the internet operates. The case also highlights ongoing tensions between copyright holders and technology providers over jurisdiction and responsibilities in digital spaces.

As a result, organizations, especially those dealing with digital content and internet services, should evaluate their compliance with regional regulations like the EU Digital Services Regulation. They might consider reinforcing partnerships with hosting providers to identify and eliminate pirated content at its source. Increasing the availability of legitimate content might also curb piracy more effectively than localized blocking measures. Internal legal teams should monitor such legal developments closely to prepare for potential similar actions and to ensure that due process rights are upheld.
