Original Article: https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/ghostposter-attacks-hide-malicious-javascript-in-firefox-addon-logos/

The 'GhostPoster' campaign involved embedding malicious JavaScript in the image logos of Firefox extensions downloaded over 50,000 times to monitor browser activities and create backdoors. This method grants attackers persistent, high-privilege access, enabling them to hijack affiliate links, inject tracking codes, and commit fraud. The campaign's stealth, using techniques like steganography and strategic payload fetching, makes it difficult for detection tools to spot. Over 17 extensions were identified as compromised, impacting users' browsers by redirecting commissions and tracking activity without the need for password harvesting.

This matter is significant due to the widespread adoption of the compromised extensions, affecting user privacy through undetected tracking and fraud activities. Steganography's use in concealing malicious code and the campaign's evasive techniques highlight evolving threats in browser security. While this specific campaign does not explicitly escalate credential theft or phishing, its potential to evolve into more severe threats underscores ongoing security risks that could compromise users further or target more sensitive information in the future.

As a response, organizations should advise users to uninstall listed malicious extensions and consider resetting passwords for critical accounts. They can also emphasize the importance of regularly updating and scrutinizing browser extensions and raising awareness about the risks tied to third-party extensions. Additionally, developing or enhancing monitoring mechanisms for suspicious activity within organizational IT environments could help detect similar threats. Firefox and other browser developers might need to continue tightening security measures against hidden, malicious payload delivery methods.
