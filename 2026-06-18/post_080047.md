**1) What happened:**  
India temporarily banned Telegram until June 22 due to its use in distributing leaked exam materials, with the issue extending inadvertently to UAE users. Telegram's CEO accused Indian telecom Reliance of using BGP hijacking to enforce the ban, affecting users far outside India. Although there were claims of sabotage due to potential business conflicts, experts agreed it was likely a misconfiguration that leaked routes globally. Telegram challenged the ban legally, while network providers were advised to manage their routing protocols to prevent such misconfigurations.

**2) Why it matters:**  
The incident highlights the broader implications of domestic internet restrictions causing unintended global outages, demonstrating potential vulnerabilities in BGP management that can affect international users. It underscores the balance between national security concerns and digital rights, as the ban, seen by some as disproportionate, disrupted legitimate educational use of Telegram. Furthermore, it emphasizes the risks to free access to services stemming from complex telecom relationships and regulatory actions, thus inspiring debates about digital sovereignty and governance.

**3) What actions should be taken:**  
Organizations should enhance BGP configurations and implement RPKI validation to prevent accidental route leaks. Government bodies might evaluate proportionate measures addressing digital infractions, focusing on collaboration with tech platforms for targeted interventions over sweeping bans. Users can explore secure methods like MTProto proxies to maintain service access during imposed restrictions. Meanwhile, policy discussions around internet governance and surveillance can be encouraged to foster frameworks that protect both national interests and digital rights globally.
