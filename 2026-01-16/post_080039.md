Original Article: https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/gootloader-now-uses-1-000-part-zip-archives-for-stealthy-delivery/

The Gootloader malware has recently evolved to use malformed ZIP files consisting of up to 1,000 parts to evade detection. This approach causes many analysis tools, except for Windows' default utility, to crash, thus allowing the malware to bypass security measures. This refined technique poses significant challenges because it's designed to break traditional detection methods through multiple obfuscation layers and continual adaptation to thwart both static and network-based detections.

The introduction of complex, multipart ZIP archives for stealthy malware delivery marks an escalation in cyber threat sophistication. It underscores an urgent need for organizations to update their detection strategies and toolsets to recognize such advanced evasion techniques. Successful exploitation may lead to unauthorized access, data breaches, and the potential deployment of ransomware, threatening operational integrity and sensitive information security.

In response to these developments, organizations could consider implementing several cybersecurity measures. They could adjust configurations to make Notepad the default handler for JScript files, reducing the likelihood of automatic malware execution. Disabling execution of scripts via `wscript.exe` and `cscript.exe` for downloaded content where JScript files aren't necessary may shrink the attack surface. Organizations may also employ detection strategies leveraging new YARA rules and examine metadata mismatches to identify anomalies indicative of Gootloader. Organizations should continually review and update incident response and detection protocols in light of evolving tactics employed by threat actors.
