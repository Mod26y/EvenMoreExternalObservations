Original Article: https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/hackers-exploit-forticlient-ems-flaw-to-push-infostealer-malware/

1) What happened: A critical vulnerability (CVE-2026-35616) in FortiClient Enterprise Management Server (EMS) has been exploited by hackers to deploy the EKZ infostealer malware. The vulnerability allows unauthorized remote execution of code, which attackers used to disguise malware as a legitimate Fortinet update. By manipulating VPN scripting workflows, the attack involved executing malicious scripts on affected endpoints, resulting in the theft of credentials and sensitive data. Fortinet released emergency fixes, while agencies like CISA quickly mandated security updates. However, unauthorized access and data exfiltration through PowerShell payloads continue to be a concern.

2) Why it matters: This incident underscores the risks posed by unpatched vulnerabilities and the potential for direct manipulation of enterprise management systems used in operational roles. The attack demonstrates how critical vulnerabilities in widely used management software can rapidly be exploited to compromise sensitive enterprise data. The EKZ infostealer’s ability to extract essential credentials and bypass password protections presents a substantial risk to organizations' security postures, potentially leading to unauthorized access, data breaches, and financial losses.

3) What actions should be taken: Organizations with FortiClient EMS should immediately review and apply updates or emergency hotfixes to versions 7.4.5 and 7.4.6. Regular audits of firewall and endpoint configurations should be carried out to identify unauthorized changes. Enhanced monitoring for anomalies in certificate authentication, administrative activities, and access configurations is recommended for early detection of similar attacks. Additionally, instituting comprehensive logging and analysis can aid in identifying the "Certificate not found in request header" indicator, allowing for prompt intervention. Strengthening user education around recognizing authentic updates versus phishing attempts is also valuable.
