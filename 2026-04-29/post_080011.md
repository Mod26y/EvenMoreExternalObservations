### What Happened
Researchers have identified a flaw in VECT 2.0 ransomware, causing it to act as a data wiper for large files rather than merely encrypting them. The issue arises from improper nonce handling, which results in permanently destroying files larger than 128KB. Although meant to speed up encryption, this flaw leaves only the last 25% of a file recoverable and renders the rest irretrievable, even if victims pay the ransom. This issue affects all VECT 2.0 variants on different platforms. VECT 2.0 is linked to supply-chain attack threat actors, expanding its operational risk.

### Why It Matters
The flaw in VECT 2.0 is particularly concerning for enterprises, as it affects files larger than a minimal 128KB threshold, covering most enterprise data such as databases, VM disks, and routine documents. This turns the ransomware into an effective data wiper, potentially causing irreversible data loss and operational disruptions. Moreover, VECT's association with supply-chain threat actors increases the risk of widespread and cascading impacts across various organizational environments. Understanding this threat helps organizations prioritize effective backup and recovery strategies, ensuring that vital data is retrievable following such destructive attacks.

### What Actions Should Be Taken
Organizations should review and enhance their backup strategies to mitigate the impact of the VECT 2.0 ransomware. Ensuring regular, comprehensive backups are stored offline could safeguard against data loss from ransomware attacks. Additionally, monitoring for BreachForums activity and threat intelligence related to TeamPCP may provide early warnings of potential supply-chain threats. Implementing robust incident response plans and educating employees about phishing and social engineering scams can reduce the risk of initial compromise, while employing advanced threat detection and patch management can curtail vulnerabilities exploited by ransomware like VECT 2.0.
