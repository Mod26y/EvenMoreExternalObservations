Original Article: https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/nist-to-stop-rating-non-priority-flaws-due-to-volume-increase/

1) What happened: The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) has decided to stop assigning severity scores to lower-priority vulnerabilities in its National Vulnerability Database (NVD) due to an overwhelming increase in the volume of submissions. Starting April 15, NIST will only provide additional details for vulnerabilities that meet specific risk-related criteria. These criteria include vulnerabilities found in CISA’s Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog, those affecting U.S. federal government software, and those involving critical software under Executive Order 14028.

2) Why it matters: This shift in NIST’s approach impacts how vulnerabilities are assessed and prioritized, potentially affecting cybersecurity strategies across various industries. The lack of detailed enrichment for lower-priority vulnerabilities might lead organizations to overlook these issues, increasing the risk of unaddressed threats. Since the NVD is widely used by security professionals and organizations for risk management, this change highlights the need for reliance on alternative sources for comprehensive assessments, thereby influencing how vulnerable software is managed and secured.

3) What actions should be taken: Organizations relying on NIST's NVD should review their vulnerability management processes and consider augmenting them with additional threat intelligence sources or tools that provide comprehensive assessments of lower-priority vulnerabilities. Engaging with industry-specific threat intelligence feeds and leveraging automation in vulnerability management may help address any gaps left by NIST’s new prioritization strategy. Additionally, proactively monitoring and regularly reviewing system architectures against known vulnerabilities to maintain a robust security posture is advisable.
