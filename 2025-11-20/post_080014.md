Original Article: https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/sneaky2fa-phaas-kit-now-uses-redteamers-browser-in-the-browser-attack/

The article reports that the Sneaky2FA phishing-as-a-service (PhaaS) kit has incorporated browser-in-the-browser (BitB) capabilities, enhancing its ability to deceive users and steal Microsoft credentials. This technique mimics legitimate Microsoft login windows by rendering a fake pop-up that adapts to the user’s operating system and browser, making it extremely convincing. The BitB attack, combined with the attacker-in-the-middle (AitM) tactics, allows criminals to bypass two-factor authentication (2FA) protections. This enhancement represents an evolution in the sophistication and effectiveness of phishing attacks against Microsoft 365 users, posing significant security risks to individuals and organizations.

This development matters because it exemplifies the increasing sophistication of phishing attacks, targeting critical Microsoft 365 accounts often used by businesses and governments. By bypassing 2FA—a common protective measure—these attacks threaten to significantly compromise sensitive data and account security. The widespread reach and adoption of the Sneaky2FA platform means that a larger number of users could be at risk, highlighting the urgent need for vigilance and enhanced security measures beyond standard authentication protocols.

Organizations should respond by training employees to recognize signs of sophisticated phishing, including the use of BitB techniques. Security teams should consider employing browser security extensions to detect and flag such pop-ups and ensure all session tokens are invalidated following initial use to mitigate AitM attacks. Enhanced detection mechanisms, like monitoring for unusual login patterns and implementing behavioral biometrics, could serve as additional defense layers. Regularly updating and educating on emerging threats can empower users against potential exploits.
